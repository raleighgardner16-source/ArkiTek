AI Hallucinations: What They Are and How to Spot Them

Feb 22, 2026 8 min read

Your AI Is Not Lying to You — But It Is Making Things Up

You ask ChatGPT for a list of academic sources on a topic. It returns five papers with titles, authors, journal names, and publication years. They look perfectly legitimate. You paste one into Google Scholar — and it doesn't exist. The author is real, the journal is real, but that specific paper was never written. The AI invented it out of thin air, formatted it to look exactly like a real citation, and presented it to you with total confidence.

This is an AI hallucination, and it's one of the most important problems to understand if you use language models for anything that matters. Hallucinations aren't rare edge cases. They happen constantly, across every major model, on every type of query. And they're dangerous precisely because they look indistinguishable from accurate output.

Understanding what hallucinations are, why they happen, and how to catch them isn't optional anymore — it's a core skill for anyone who works with AI. This article breaks down the mechanics, the common types, and a practical detection workflow that dramatically reduces the risk of acting on fabricated information.

What AI Hallucinations Actually Are

The term "hallucination" is slightly misleading because it implies the AI is experiencing something analogous to a human delusion. It's not. What's actually happening is far more mundane — and in some ways, more concerning.

Large language models like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini don't retrieve facts from a database. They predict the most likely next word (technically, next token) based on patterns learned during training. When you ask "Who wrote Hamlet?", the model doesn't look up the answer. It calculates that, given the sequence of tokens in your prompt, the highest-probability next tokens form the string "William Shakespeare." For well-known facts, this statistical prediction aligns with reality. For less common questions, the prediction can produce text that is grammatically perfect, stylistically confident, and completely wrong.

Hallucinations are not bugs in the traditional software sense. They are a fundamental property of how autoregressive language models work. The model is always generating plausible-sounding text. Sometimes plausible and true happen to coincide. Sometimes they don't. The model has no internal mechanism to distinguish between the two.

The Five Types of AI Hallucinations

Not all hallucinations look the same. Recognizing the different types helps you know where to be most vigilant.

1. Fabricated Citations and Sources

This is the most well-documented type. Ask any model to provide academic references, and there's a meaningful probability that some of them are partially or entirely invented. The model generates what a citation should look like — a plausible author name, a journal that publishes on the topic, a title that sounds right — without any grounding in actual published work. A New York lawyer famously submitted a legal brief containing six fabricated case citations generated by ChatGPT, none of which existed. The model had invented case names, docket numbers, and even judicial opinions. This type of hallucination is especially dangerous in academic research and legal work because the output follows the exact format of legitimate sources.

2. Confident False Facts

The model states something specific and verifiable — a date, a statistic, a historical event, a biographical detail — and gets it wrong. Not approximately wrong, but specifically and confidently wrong. It might tell you a company was founded in 2012 when it was actually 2014. It might claim a country has a population of 45 million when the real number is 32 million. It might attribute a quote to the wrong person. The confidence is the problem. The model presents these facts with the same tone and certainty it uses for information it gets right, giving you no signal that this particular claim needs verification.

3. Plausible-Sounding Nonsense

This is the subtlest type and perhaps the hardest to catch. The model produces text that is technically coherent — proper grammar, logical sentence structure, domain-appropriate vocabulary — but the actual content is meaningless or factually vacuous. You might ask about a niche scientific mechanism and receive a paragraph that uses all the right terminology but describes a process that doesn't exist. It reads like an answer. It sounds like an answer. But it conveys no real information. This type of hallucination is particularly common in specialized domains where the model's training data was sparse.

4. Outdated Information Presented as Current

Every model has a training data cutoff. Information that was accurate when the model was trained may no longer be true. But the model rarely flags this. It will tell you that a particular CEO still leads a company months after they stepped down. It will describe a software library's API using syntax that was deprecated two versions ago. It will cite statistics from a study that has since been retracted or superseded. The hallucination here is not that the information was never true — it's that the model presents it as current when it isn't, and the user has no way to know from the response alone.

5. Entity Confusion

Models frequently mix up similar entities — people who share a last name, companies in the same industry, cities with the same name in different states or countries, scientific concepts with overlapping terminology. Ask about a moderately well-known person and you might get a response that blends biographical details from two different individuals. Ask about a specific software framework and you might get features described that actually belong to a competitor. The model compresses related concepts into overlapping probability distributions, and when it generates text, it sometimes pulls attributes from the wrong cluster.

Why Hallucinations Happen

Several factors contribute to the frequency and severity of hallucinations, and understanding them helps explain why no single model has solved the problem.

Temperature and sampling settings play a role. Higher temperature values introduce more randomness into token selection, which increases creativity but also increases the probability of generating tokens that don't correspond to factual reality. Even at lower temperatures, the model can hallucinate when the "correct" token doesn't have a dominant probability in the distribution.

Training data gaps are a major factor. Models are trained on enormous but finite datasets. For topics that are underrepresented in the training data — niche academic fields, recent events, small companies, local knowledge — the model has fewer examples to ground its predictions in. It still generates output because that's what it's designed to do, but the output is more likely to be invented rather than recalled.

The people-pleasing tendency of RLHF-trained models (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) is an underappreciated cause. During fine-tuning, models are rewarded for producing responses that human evaluators rate as helpful and complete. This creates an incentive to always provide an answer, even when the model "doesn't know" the answer. Instead of saying "I'm not sure," the model generates a plausible-sounding response because that's the behavior that was reinforced during training. The model would rather be confidently wrong than helpfully uncertain.

Which Models Hallucinate — and How

Every model hallucinates, but they hallucinate differently. If you've spent time on the ChatGPT vs Claude comparison, you've likely noticed these patterns firsthand.

ChatGPT tends toward confident over-specificity. When it hallucinates, it often invents very specific details — exact dates, precise statistics, named sources — that feel authoritative but are fabricated. This is particularly dangerous because specificity is one of the cues humans use to judge credibility.

Claude tends to be more cautious and is more likely to hedge or express uncertainty. However, when Claude does hallucinate, it can be harder to detect because the hedging language creates a false sense that the model is being transparent about its limitations. You might read "This appears to be the case, though I'd recommend verifying" and assume the underlying information is correct, when it isn't.

Gemini has access to Google's search infrastructure in some configurations, which reduces certain types of hallucinations. But it can still hallucinate on niche topics, particularly when search grounding doesn't activate or when the question requires synthesis across multiple sources rather than simple retrieval.

Grok, DeepSeek, Llama, and Mistral each have their own hallucination profiles shaped by their training data, architecture choices, and fine-tuning approaches. No model is immune, and the specific failure modes vary enough that what one model gets wrong, another often gets right.

The Multi-Model Detection Method

This is where the problem becomes solvable — or at least manageable. The single most effective technique for catching hallucinations is to send the same query to multiple independent models and compare the responses. This is the core principle behind why relying on one AI model is never enough.

When four models answer the same factual question and all four agree, your confidence in the answer should be high. The probability that four independently trained models would all hallucinate the same false information in the same way is extremely low. Their training data differs, their architectures differ, and their biases differ. Agreement across models is a strong signal of accuracy.

When models disagree, you've found a potential hallucination. If three models say a company was founded in 2015 and one says 2013, you know to verify. If two models cite a particular statistic and two others give a different number, that's a red flag worth investigating. The disagreement itself is the signal — and it's a signal you'd never get from querying a single model.

5 Red Flags That an AI Response Might Contain Hallucinations

Even before you cross-reference with other models, these warning signs should trigger your verification instinct:

  • Suspiciously specific details you didn't ask for. If the model volunteers very precise statistics, exact dates, or named sources unprompted, verify them. Real knowledge and hallucinated knowledge both feel specific, but hallucinations often over-specify to sound credible.
  • Claims about recent events or current state. Anything referencing the current status of a company, person, technology, or market should be treated as potentially outdated. Models freeze at their training cutoff, but they rarely tell you that.
  • Niche domain knowledge delivered with high confidence. If you're asking about a specialized topic and the model responds as though it's an expert with no hedging, be skeptical. Genuine expertise and hallucinated expertise are hard to distinguish, but overconfidence on obscure topics is a red flag.
  • Answers that are exactly what you wanted to hear. RLHF-trained models are optimized to be helpful. If the response feels like it's telling you what you want to hear rather than what's actually true, the people-pleasing bias may be at work.
  • Inability to provide a source or explain reasoning when pressed. If you ask the model to cite its source and it either can't or provides a vague reference, the underlying claim is suspect. Accurate information usually has a traceable provenance; hallucinated information doesn't.

A Practical Hallucination-Catching Workflow

Here's how to structure your AI usage to minimize the risk of acting on hallucinated information:

Step 1: Query multiple models simultaneously. Use ArkitekAI to send your question to all available models at once. This takes no more time than querying a single model and gives you the raw material for comparison.

Step 2: Read the AI Judge summary first. The Judge automatically flags areas of disagreement between models. Start here rather than reading every response in full — the Judge highlights exactly where you need to pay attention.

Step 3: Investigate disagreements. When models contradict each other, don't assume the majority is right. Use the disagreement as a research prompt — look up the specific claim yourself. The disagreement has told you exactly what to verify, saving you from having to fact-check everything.

Step 4: Apply the red flags filter. For claims that all models agree on but that trigger any of the red flags above, do a quick external verification anyway. Consensus is strong evidence but not proof.

Step 5: For high-stakes content, verify externally. If you're writing a legal brief, submitting academic work, publishing financial analysis, or making a major business decision, the AI-assisted comparison is a starting point, not a finishing point. Use it to identify what needs verification, then verify through authoritative primary sources.

For students using AI for academic work, this workflow is especially important. Academic integrity depends on the accuracy of your sources, and submitting a paper built on hallucinated citations is a serious problem that multi-model verification can largely prevent.

Why This Matters More Than Most People Think

The consequences of trusting hallucinated AI output are already piling up. Lawyers have been sanctioned for filing briefs with invented case law. Students have submitted papers with fabricated sources. Businesses have made decisions based on market data that didn't exist. Journalists have published articles containing AI-generated "facts" that were never true.

As AI usage accelerates, the volume of hallucinated information entering the real world grows with it. Every person who copies an AI response into a document without verification is potentially introducing false information into their work, their organization, and — if they publish or share it — the broader information ecosystem.

The fix isn't to stop using AI. The models are too useful, too fast, and too capable to abandon. The fix is to change how you use AI — from single-model trust to multi-model verification. The same way you wouldn't make a major financial decision based on one advisor's opinion, you shouldn't trust a single AI model's output on anything that matters.

Hallucinations are not going away. They're a structural feature of how language models work, and while each new model generation reduces their frequency, no model has eliminated them. The only reliable defense is cross-verification — and the easier it is to cross-verify, the more likely people are to actually do it.

AI hallucinations aren't a bug that will be patched in the next update. They're a fundamental property of language models. The question isn't whether your AI will hallucinate — it's whether you'll catch it when it does.
